Evidence from\n1 Introduction\nBanks' capital ratios have received much attention because banks tend to have capital ratios\nby far lower than industrials, and a failure of a systemically relevant bank may threaten to\nderail the economy as a whole. Banks face a trade-off when choosing the appropriate level of\ntheir capital ratio. On the one hand, regulatory authorities and rating agencies force the banks\nto maintain a minimum capital ratio. The regulatory lower limit for the total-capital ratio is 8\npercent, while rating agencies and other market participants insist that a bank holds a certain\nratio of Tier 1 capital if it wants to obtain a certain rating. On the other hand, banks try to\nmaximize their return on capital to satisfy their investors; in contradiction to Modigliani/Mil-\nler's irrelevance theorem (1958), it is believed that banks can increase their performance by\nsubstituting capital with debt.\nWe pose the following three research questions: (1) Do banks adjust their capital ratios to a\npredefined target level or does the capital ratio fluctuate randomly, driven only by stochastic\nshocks without tendency to a mean? (2) Which bank characteristics determine whether banks\nadjust their capital ratio? (3) In our setting, the probability of failing to meet the regulatory\nrequirements depends on three strategic parameters: the target capital ratio, the adjustment\nrate, and the asset volatility. Is there a compensating relationship between these three parame-\nters, for instance do we find that banks with a high capital cushion have volatile assets and\nlow adjustment speeds?\nOur contribution to the literature is twofold. First, we are the first to estimate a partial adjust-\nment model for the capital ratio that determines the adjustment rate for each bank separately.\nUsing monthly (instead of yearly or at best quarterly) data, we can apply the tools of time\nseries analysis, especially those of stationarity analysis. Second, we provide insights into the\nstrategic behavior of the capital management of German banks.\n1 "
