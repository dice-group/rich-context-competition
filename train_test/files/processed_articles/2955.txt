The theoret-\nical analysis suggests two different regimes, depending on whether investments are direct or\nindirect. One way to approach this problem is to consider direct and indirect investments\nas two separate samples, simply split observations, and estimate two distinct equations.\nHowever, firms do not randomly choose one or the other regime, and a simple split does\nnot fully account for this endogeneity.11\nTo address this endogeneity problem we follow the literature and estimate an endogenous\nswitching regression model, where the switching rule is observed (Lee, 1978). Maddala\n(1983) suggests a two-step procedure: first, estimate the binary variable I, indicating\nwhether the investment is directly or indirectly held. Subsequently, estimate affiliate in-\nvestment in a second stage and condition on the regime choice.\nFrom a taxation perspective, conduit structures should generally be dominant, empirically.\n11We can think of endogeneity in this context in many different ways. First, there are some firms switch-\ning from one regime to the other. The ultimate regression sample contains exactly 50 affiliates switching\nat least once over the whole time span. Firms switching and immediately switching back, however, do\nnot contribute new information if they come up as single observations in the panel data regressions with\naffiliate-specific effects. Second, firms' potentials to cushion shocks differ, depending on the regime. Hence,\nthe regime is correlated with these shocks.
